3. GOOGLE WORKSPACE MARKETPLACE — APP VERIFICATION
================================================================================
   Priority: HIGH (only if listing on Marketplace)
   Who: [ONLINE] — you do most of this yourself through Google's console,
        but the security assessment part may require a paid third-party firm

   Steps you do yourself:
     1. Go to Google Cloud Console → APIs & Services → OAuth consent screen
     2. Fill in app name, support email, developer contact, privacy policy
        URL, terms of service URL (from step 2 above)
     3. Add the scopes from appsscript.json
     4. Submit for verification

   What Google may require:
     - For sensitive scopes (gmail.modify, gmail.send, gmail.readonly):
       Google will likely require a security assessment by an approved
       third-party assessor. Current approved firms are listed at:
         https://support.google.com/cloud/answer/9110914
     - Cost: typically $2,000–$15,000 depending on the assessor and scope.
     - Timeline: 2–6 weeks for the assessment, then Google's review on top.

   You do NOT need a lawyer for this — it's a technical/process step. But
   if Google's reviewer asks about your privacy policy or data handling,
   having a lawyer-reviewed PRIVACY.md makes that go smoothly.

4. TRADEMARK — "MAILALERT"
================================================================================
   Priority: MEDIUM
   Who: Steps 1-2 are [ONLINE] (you do it yourself). Step 3 depends on
        what you find — may need [LAWYER] if it's complicated.

   Step 1 — Search (free, do it yourself):
     1. Go to https://tess2.uspto.gov (USPTO trademark search)
     2. Click "Basic Word Mark Search (New User)"
     3. Search for: mAIl Alert
     4. Also search for: Mail Alert (two words)
     5. Look at results in International Class 009 (software) and
        International Class 042 (SaaS / software services)
     6. If you find existing registrations that are live (not dead/cancelled)
        and cover software — that's a conflict. Stop and talk to a lawyer.
     7. If the search comes back clean or only shows dead marks, proceed.

   Step 2 — File the application yourself (if search is clean):
     1. Go to https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/apply
     2. Choose TEAS Standard ($350/class) or TEAS Plus ($250/class,
        more restrictive on goods/services description)
     3. You'll need:
        - The mark: "mAIl Alert" (standard characters)
        - Owner: JJJJJ Enterprises, LLC
        - Class: 009 (computer software) and/or 042 (SaaS)
        - Goods/services description, e.g.: "Downloadable computer
          software in the nature of a Gmail add-on for monitoring email
          and sending alerts based on user-defined rules"
        - Filing basis: 1(a) "use in commerce" if the product is live,
          or 1(b) "intent to use" if not yet launched
        - A specimen showing the mark in use (screenshot of the add-on
          with "mAIl Alert" visible) — required for 1(a), deferred for 1(b)
     4. Pay and submit. Timeline: 3-4 months for an examining attorney
        to review. If approved, published for 30-day opposition period.
        Total timeline to registration: 8-12 months if no issues.

   Step 3 — When you need a lawyer:
     If you get an "Office Action" (rejection or request for clarification)
     from the USPTO examining attorney, that's when to call a trademark
     attorney. Common issues:
       - "Likelihood of confusion" with an existing mark
       - "Merely descriptive" refusal (mAIl Alert describes what it does)
     A trademark attorney typically charges $500–$1,500 to respond to an
     Office Action.

   What to ask a trademark lawyer:
     "I want to register the trademark 'mAIl Alert' for a Gmail add-on
     that monitors email and sends alerts. The LLC is JJJJJ Enterprises.
     I've done a TESS search and [found / didn't find] potential conflicts.
     Can you:
       (a) do a comprehensive search (including common law and state marks),
       (b) advise on whether the mark is registrable given it may be
           considered descriptive, and
       (c) file the application and handle any Office Actions?"

5. HAVE A LAWYER REVIEW THE LEGAL DOCS
================================================================================
   Priority: HIGH (before publishing to anyone outside your org)
   Who: [LAWYER]

   The LICENSE, TERMS.md, PRIVACY.md, and DISCLAIMER.md in this repo are
   AI-generated templates. They cover the right topics but have not been
   reviewed by a licensed attorney. A lawyer needs to:
     - Confirm they're enforceable in your state
     - Tighten language that may be too vague
     - Add anything specific to your business situation

   What to ask for (send them the four files and say this):

     "I operate a Google Workspace Gmail Add-on called mAIl Alert through
     my LLC (JJJJJ Enterprises, LLC, formed in [STATE]). The add-on runs
     inside the user's own Google account — I don't operate a backend or
     store user data on my servers. It reads their Gmail, sends email
     content to the Google Gemini API for AI evaluation, and can send
     alerts via the user's Gmail and optionally via Twilio SMS.

     I need you to review and finalize these four documents:

     1. LICENSE — Proprietary software license. Confirm it's enforceable
        and that making the source code publicly visible on GitHub does
        NOT constitute an implied license grant.

     2. TERMS.md — Terms of Service. Specifically:
        - Is the limitation of liability cap ($100) appropriate and
          enforceable in [STATE]?
        - Should I use an arbitration clause instead of court jurisdiction
          (Section 15)? What are the tradeoffs?
        - Is the indemnification clause (Section 11) enforceable against
          consumers in [STATE]?
        - Are there any mandatory consumer protection disclosures required
          in [STATE] that I'm missing?

     3. PRIVACY.md — Privacy Policy. Specifically:
        - Does it satisfy Google's API Services User Data Policy
          (the 'Limited Use' requirements)?
        - Is the GDPR section adequate, or do I need a more detailed
          data processing description if EU users install it?
        - Does the CCPA section need a 'Do Not Sell My Personal
          Information' link even though I don't sell data?

     4. DISCLAIMER.md — No-warranty / AI accuracy disclaimer. Specifically:
        - Is the AI accuracy disclaimer strong enough to protect against
          a claim that a missed alert caused harm?
        - Should the 'no life-safety use' prohibition be in a separate
          click-through agreement?

     My budget for this review is roughly [$$$ - state your budget].
     Please quote a flat fee to review, mark up, and finalize all four."

   Where to find a lawyer:
     - LegalZoom (legalzoom.com) — attorney review packages, $200-$500
       for document review
     - Rocket Lawyer (rocketlawyer.com) — similar, subscription model
     - Your state bar association's lawyer referral service (usually a
       $35 consultation fee): google "[STATE] bar lawyer referral"
     - Upwork / Contra — freelance attorneys, $100-$300/hr, good for
       one-off reviews
     - If you already have a business attorney from forming the LLC,
       start there

   Expected cost: $300–$1,500 depending on the attorney and thoroughness.

     1. Go to https://cla-assistant.io
     2. Sign in with GitHub
     3. Link your StephenRJohns/mailalert repo
     4. It will ask you to provide the CLA text. Use this template:

        ---
        Individual Contributor License Agreement

        By submitting a pull request or other contribution ("Contribution")
        to the mAIl Alert project, you agree to the following terms:

        1. You grant JJJJJ Enterprises, LLC a perpetual, worldwide,
           non-exclusive, royalty-free, irrevocable license to use,
           reproduce, modify, distribute, sublicense, and otherwise
           exploit your Contribution, in any form, for any purpose.

        2. You represent that you are the sole author of the Contribution
           and have the legal right to grant this license.

        3. You understand that your Contribution will be incorporated into
           proprietary software and that you will receive no compensation.
        ---

     5. Every PR author will be asked to sign before merging.

   Option B — A lawyer writes a proper CLA:
     Only needed if you expect corporate contributors or want assignment
     (full copyright transfer) instead of a license grant.

     What to ask a lawyer:
       "I need a Contributor License Agreement for my proprietary software
       project. Contributors submit code via GitHub pull requests. I want
       [copyright assignment / a broad license grant] so that JJJJJ
       Enterprises, LLC owns or can freely use all contributed code.
       Please draft an individual CLA and a corporate CLA."

     Expected cost: $300–$800.

7. INSURANCE
================================================================================
   Priority: MEDIUM
   Who: [ONLINE] — you buy this yourself through an insurance broker or
        online portal. No lawyer needed.

   What to get:
     a) Errors & Omissions (E&O) / Professional Liability
        - Covers claims that your software caused financial harm
          (e.g. "your add-on missed an alert and I lost a deal")
        - Typical cost for a small software LLC: $500–$1,500/year
        - Typical coverage: $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate

     b) Cyber Liability (optional, but smart)
        - Covers data breach response, notification costs, regulatory fines
        - Typical cost: $500–$1,000/year for a small software LLC
        - Less critical for mAIl Alert since you don't store user data,
          but still protects if a Twilio key leak or similar occurs

   Where to buy:
     - Hiscox (hiscox.com) — online quotes for tech/software businesses,
       you can get a quote and buy in 15 minutes
     - Hartford (thehartford.com) — same
     - Embroker (embroker.com) — specializes in tech startups
     - A local insurance broker — they shop multiple carriers for you

   Steps:
     1. Go to one of the above sites
     2. Select "Technology" or "Software" as your business type
     3. Select "Errors & Omissions / Professional Liability"
     4. Fill in: revenue (even if $0 right now), number of employees,
        what the software does (email monitoring add-on)
     5. Get a quote → buy the policy → save the certificate of insurance

9. EXPORT CONTROL / ENCRYPTION NOTICE
================================================================================
   Priority: LOW
   Who: [DIY] for the self-classification. [LAWYER] if you add real
        encryption features later.

   Current status: mAIl Alert uses HTTPS (TLS) for all external calls but
   does not implement its own encryption. Under BIS/EAR rules, using only
   standard TLS libraries qualifies for the TSU exception (EAR 740.13(e)).
   No filing is required.

   What you can do yourself right now:
     Nothing — you're already in the clear. Just don't add custom
     encryption (AES, PGP, etc.) without revisiting this.

   When you'd need a lawyer:
     If you later add end-to-end encryption for stored rules, encrypted
     backup/export, or PGP-signed alerts. At that point:

     What to ask:
       "I'm adding [describe encryption feature] to my software product.
       It's distributed as a Google Workspace Add-on, potentially to users
       outside the U.S. Do I need a BIS self-classification (CCATS) or an
       encryption registration? What ECCN does this fall under?"

     Expected cost: $500–$2,000 for an export control opinion letter.

10. DATA PROCESSING AGREEMENT (DPA)
================================================================================
    Priority: LOW (only if selling to enterprise or EU customers)
    Who: [LAWYER] — a DPA is a legal contract, not a checkbox

    Not needed today. Only relevant if enterprise customers or their legal
    teams ask "where's your DPA?" during procurement.

    Why it's complicated for mAIl Alert:
      You don't operate a backend. The user's data stays in their Google
      account. Arguably you're not a "data processor" at all — the user
      processes their own data. But enterprise legal teams don't always
      care about that distinction.

    When the time comes, what to ask a lawyer:
      "Enterprise customers are asking for a Data Processing Agreement.
      My software is a Google Workspace Add-on that runs entirely in the
      customer's own Google account. I don't operate a server or database.
      Email content is sent from the user's account to Google's Gemini API
      and optionally to Twilio for SMS.

      I need:
        (a) An opinion on whether I'm a 'data processor' under GDPR
            given this architecture.
        (b) If yes, a DPA template I can offer to enterprise customers
            that accurately reflects what I do and don't control.
        (c) Standard contractual clauses (SCCs) if needed for
            cross-border transfers."

    Expected cost: $1,000–$3,000 for a DPA template.

12. OPEN-SOURCE LICENSING AUDIT
================================================================================
    Priority: LOW
    Who: [DIY] — only needed if you add dependencies later

    Right now mAIl Alert is pure Apps Script with zero third-party libraries.
    There is nothing to audit. If you ever add bundled libraries:

    Steps:
      1. Install license-checker: npm install -g license-checker
      2. Run: license-checker --summary
      3. Look for anything that is GPL, AGPL, or SSPL — those are
         "copyleft" licenses that would require you to open-source your
         own code if you distribute it combined with theirs.
      4. MIT, BSD, Apache-2.0, ISC are all fine with proprietary software.
      5. If you find a copyleft dependency, either remove it or replace it
         with a permissively-licensed alternative.
